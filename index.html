BREAKING NEWS!!!

PETER THE HERMIT HAS STARTED A HOLY PEOPLE'S CRUSADE TO JERUSALEM!

By Parham Darziani

Published on October 16, 1096

Peter the Hermit has started a holy people's Crusade to Jerusalem from Cologne, Germany. He announced a few days ago to the people of Cologne that they will receive absolution of all sins if they join, he has invited any Crusader to join his Crusade.


Sometime around April of 1096, Peter the Hermit proclaimed a Crusade in Cologne, Germany. Colognians erected tents in Cologne and traced the course of the Rhine River for several days until they traversed it and the Rhine Valley.

From there, they ascended the Neckar River until they reached the Danube River.

The Danube facilitated their passage around the formidable mountains that separated them from the Byzantine Empire. Upon encountering a river, the Crusaders embarked in wooden vessels until they arrived at a village where the Hermit decided to halt and rest.

Due to insufficient provisions throughout the camp, nocturnal thefts occurred regularly.

Walter Sans Avoir, a nobleman who initiated the Crusade two weeks prior to Peter the Hermit, had some of his men pilfer a bazaar in Semlin and faced retribution. This incensed Walter and triggered severe retaliation.

When Walter and his men reached Belgrade, they ruthlessly massacred the inhabitants.

Several of Walter's men were captured and immolated in a church as the townspeople were consumed with anger over Walter's actions. After traversing with their wagons, the Crusaders reached Hungary. Following an incident where a Hungarian villager "robbed" a soldier, many soldiers commenced indiscriminate killings, resulting in 4,000 deaths within the city. In light of these events, the Crusaders abstained from making camp that night and instead reached the Sava River, positioning the Danube between them and the Hungarians.

Once they arrived in Belgrade, they encountered an abandoned city and seized the opportunity to plunder it entirely.

After departing from the Danube, the Crusaders set their sights on Constantinople and arrived in Nish within seven days. Despite their actions, the city's governor permitted the Crusaders to camp outside the city for a few days and even supplied them with provisions. In return, he temporarily held two Crusaders as hostagesâ€”Geoffrey Burel and Walter of Breteuil. Once the two hostages were released, they were prepared to resume the Crusade. However, a group of inebriated individuals set fire to a mill, which provoked the ire of Nicetas. Nicetas's soldiers massacred many of the Crusaders.

Finally, amidst the summer heat, the Crusaders reached Constantinople over three months after their departure from Cologne. They were met by Emperor Alexius of the Byzantine Empire, and they were captivated by the resplendent beauty of Constantinople.

Rumors circulated that Emperor Alexius had provided Peter with funds for the remainder of the Crusade, which left some Crusaders dissatisfied, believing that Peter should have received more. The Crusaders ransacked palaces and villas, looting whatever they could find. Alexius recognized this and deemed it necessary for them to depart.

One morning, the camp awoke to the clamor of Walter Sans Avoir and his soldiers arriving.

On another morning, news spread of Crusaders sacking villages in Nicaea.

The incensed Turks marched towards Civetot, where the Crusaders and Turks would engage in battle.

The conflict was decided by a charge of charging horses between the two forces.

The Turks proceeded to slay almost all of the Crusaders, including Count Emil.

This Crusade concluded around October, spanning approximately seven months.
